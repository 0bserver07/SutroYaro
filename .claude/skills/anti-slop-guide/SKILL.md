# Anti-Slop Guide

Field guide for eliminating AI writing patterns from prose. Compiled from multiple sources including Wikipedia's Signs of AI Writing, community research, and practitioner guides.

---

## Part 1: Core Rules

1. **Cut filler phrases.** Remove throat-clearing openers and emphasis crutches. Say the thing directly.
2. **Break formulaic structures.** Avoid binary contrasts, dramatic fragmentation, rhetorical setups.
3. **Vary rhythm.** Mix sentence lengths. Two items beat three. End paragraphs differently each time.
4. **Trust readers.** State facts directly. Skip softening, justification, hand-holding.
5. **Cut quotables.** If it sounds like a pull-quote or motivational poster, rewrite it.
6. **Be specific.** Replace generic statements with concrete details. Specificity is the antidote to slop.
7. **Prefer plain verbs.** Use "said" not "emphasized," "used" not "leveraged," "showed" not "showcased."
8. **Use simple copulatives.** Write "X is Y" instead of "X serves as Y" or "X stands as Y."
9. **Kill the em-dash habit.** Use commas or periods where LLMs reflexively reach for em dashes.
10. **Never triple.** Two items in a list, not three. Three-item lists are the most reliable AI tell.

## Part 2: Overused "AI Vocabulary" Words

These words started appearing far more frequently in text after 2023. They co-occur in LLM output; where there is one, there are likely others. Introducing lots of them is one of the strongest tells for AI use.

### Tier 1: Strongest Tells (almost certainly AI if clustered)

additionally, align with, crucial, delve, emphasizing, enduring, enhance, fostering, garner, highlight (as a verb), interplay, intricate/intricacies, key (as an adjective), landscape (abstract noun), pivotal, showcase, tapestry (abstract noun), testament, underscore (as a verb), valuable, vibrant

### Tier 2: Strong Tells (very common in AI output)

- bustling, comprehensive, multifaceted, nuanced, paramount
- realm, robust, seamless/seamlessly, synergy, transformative
- commendable, cornerstone, groundbreaking, innovative, noteworthy
- spearhead, streamline, testament to, trailblazing, unwavering

### Tier 3: Suspicious in Clusters

- bolster, burgeoning, captivate, cater to, cognizant
- compelling, culminate, delineate, demystify, discern
- elucidate, embark, encompass, envisage, epitomize
- equip, evolving, exemplify, expedite, facilitate
- fathom, forge, foster, grapple, harness
- illuminate, imbue, imperative, indispensable, ingenious
- juxtapose, meticulous, mitigate, navigate, nestle
- optimize, orchestrate, paradigm, perpetuate, pervasive
- plethora, poignant, proliferate, propel, quintessential
- reimagine, resonate, revolutionize, rigorous, salient
- scrutinize, sentiment, testament, transcend, traverse
- underscore, unprecedented, unravel, utilize, volition

### AI-Overused Intensifiers

deeply, truly, fundamentally, inherently, simply, literally, inevitably, remarkably, notably, particularly, essentially, undeniably, profoundly

### Absolute Words (false authority)

always, never, everyone, everybody, nobody, undoubtedly, unquestionably

## Part 3: Phrases to Remove

### Throat-Clearing Openers

Remove these. State the content directly.

- "Here's the thing:"
- "The uncomfortable truth is"
- "It turns out"
- "The real [X] is"
- "Let me be clear"
- "The truth is,"
- "I'll say it again:"
- "I'm going to be honest"
- "Can we talk about"
- "Here's what I find interesting"
- "Here's the problem though"
- "But here's the thing"
- "Something shifted"
- "Everything changed"

### Emphasis Crutches

These add no meaning. Delete them.

- "Full stop." / "Period."
- "Let that sink in."
- "This matters because"
- "Make no mistake"
- "Here's why that matters"
- "Think about it."
- "Read that again."
- "I cannot stress this enough"

### Generic Essay Openers

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "In today's [X]..."
- "As we navigate the complexities..."
- "In a world where..."
- "As technology continues to evolve..."
- "In the ever-evolving landscape of..."

### Generic Essay Closers

- "In conclusion..."
- "In summary..."
- "Overall..."
- "As [X] continues to evolve..."
- "The future of [X] is bright"
- "Only time will tell"

### Meta-Commentary

Remove self-referential asides:

- "Hint:"
- "Plot twist:" / "Spoiler:"
- "You already know this, but"
- "But that's another post"
- "X is a feature, not a bug"
- "Dressed up as"
- "In this section, we'll look at..."
- "As mentioned earlier..."
- "Now that we've explored X..."
- "First, we will examine... then we will explore..."

### Performative Emphasis

False intimacy or manufactured sincerity:

- "creeps in"
- "I promise"
- "They exist, I promise"
- "This is genuinely hard"
- "This is what leadership actually looks like"
- "This is what X actually looks like"

### Filler Adverbs and Transitions

Cut or replace:

- "At its core"
- "It's worth noting"
- "Interestingly,"
- "Importantly,"
- "Crucially,"
- "At the end of the day"
- "When it comes to"
- "The reality is"
- "Furthermore," / "Moreover," / "In addition," (when stacked)
- "It's important to note/remember/consider"
- "Worth noting"

### Collaborative Communication Leakage

These appear when chatbot-to-user phrasing leaks into prose:

- "I hope this helps"
- "Of course!"
- "Certainly!"
- "You're absolutely right!"
- "Would you like..."
- "Is there anything else"
- "Let me know"
- "Here is a..."
- "Great question!"

### Politeness Padding

- "I hope this email finds you well..."
- "I would like to take a moment to..."
- "I was wondering if..."
- "I wanted to kindly follow up regarding..."
- Excessive "just," "hopefully," "perhaps"

## Part 5: Structural Patterns to Avoid

### Binary Contrasts

These create false drama. State the point directly.

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "Not because X. Because Y." | Telegraphed reversal |
| "[X] isn't the problem. [Y] is." | Formulaic reframe |
| "The answer isn't X. It's Y." | Predictable pivot |
| "It feels like X. It's actually Y." | Setup/reveal cliche |
| "The question isn't X. It's Y." | Rhetorical misdirection |
| "Not X. But Y." | Mechanical contrast |
| "It's not this. It's that." | Same formula, different words |
| "stops being X and starts being Y" | False transformation arc |
| "It isn't just X, it's Y" | Promotional reframe |
| "X is more than just Y; it's Z" | Escalation cliche |
| "It wasn't X, it was Y" | Hindsight reversal |
| "This is where X comes in" | Manufactured entrance |

**Fix:** State Y directly. "The problem is Y." "Y matters here."

### Dramatic Fragmentation

Sentence fragments for emphasis read as manufactured profundity.

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "[Noun]. That's it. That's the [thing]." | Performative simplicity |
| "X. And Y. And Z." | Staccato drama |
| "This unlocks something. [Word]." | Artificial revelation |

**Fix:** Complete sentences. Trust content over presentation.

### Rhetorical Setups

These announce insight rather than deliver it.

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "What if [reframe]?" | Socratic posturing |
| "Here's what I mean:" | Redundant preview |
| "Think about it:" | Condescending prompt |
| "And that's okay." | Unnecessary permission |
| "So, what exactly is X?" | Fake curiosity |
| "But now? You won't believe..." | Manufactured suspense |

**Fix:** Make the point. Let readers draw conclusions.

### Negative Parallelisms

Parallel constructions involving "not", "but", or "however" such as "Not only ... but ..." or "It is not just about ..., it's ..." are very common in LLM writing to appear balanced and thoughtful.

- "not ..., it's ..."
- "no ..., no ..., just ..."
- "Not only X, but also Y"

### Rule of Three

LLMs overuse the rule of three. This takes different forms:
- "adjective, adjective, adjective"
- "short phrase, short phrase, and short phrase"
- Three-item lists to make superficial analyses appear comprehensive
- Three examples where one or two would suffice

**Fix:** Use two items or one. Break the triple pattern.

### False Ranges

LLMs employ figurative "from ... to ..." constructions where the endpoints are loosely related or unrelated and no meaningful scale can be inferred.

- "from boardrooms to battlefields"
- "from grassroots to global"
- "from problem-solving to scientific discovery"

**Fix:** Be specific about what you actually mean.

### Formulaic Constructions

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "By the time X, I was Y." | Narrative template |
| "X that isn't Y" | Indirect. Say "X is broken" |
| "Despite its [positive], X faces challenges..." | Outline-like conclusion |
| "Challenges and Legacy" | AI section heading cliche |
| "Future Outlook" / "Future Prospects" | Speculative padding |

### The "Challenges" Formula

Many AI articles include a section that begins "Despite its [positive quality], [subject] faces challenges..." and ends with vaguely positive speculation. This rigid formula is a strong AI tell.

## Part 6: Rhythm and Style Patterns

### Sentence-Level Issues

| Pattern | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Three consecutive sentences match length | Break one |
| Paragraph ends with punchy one-liner | Vary endings |
| Em-dash before a reveal | Remove it, use period or comma |
| Explaining a metaphor | Trust it to land |
| Questions answered immediately | Let questions breathe or cut them |
| Paragraphs starting with "So" | Start with content |
| Sentences starting with "Look," | Remove |
| Every paragraph ends punchily | Vary endings |
| Staccato fragmentation | Don't stack short punchy sentences |
| Dashes for dramatic pause | Use commas or periods |
| "Not always. Not perfectly." | Hedging disguised as reassurance |
| Multiple sentences opening identically | Vary first 3-5 words |
| Uniform paragraph length | Mix short and long |
| Reusing "Furthermore/Moreover/In addition" | Use one, cut the rest, or restructure |

### Formatting Tells

- **Overuse of boldface**: Mechanical emphasis in a "key takeaways" fashion
- **Emoji in headings/bullets**: Decorating section headings or bullet points with emoji
- **Inline-header vertical lists**: Bold header followed by colon, then description text
- **Excessive bullet points**: Replacing developed paragraphs with bulleted lists
- **Unnecessary tables**: Small tables that could be stated as prose
- **Title Case in headings**: Capitalizing All Main Words in headings (prefer sentence case)
- **Decorative symbols**: Unnecessary arrows (->), check marks, or Unicode styling
- **Skipping heading levels**: Jumping from H1 to H3

### Copulative Avoidance

LLMs substitute constructions like "serves as a" or "marks the" for simpler "is" or "are" phrasing. One study documented an over 10% decrease in "is" and "are" usage in AI-assisted academic writing.

| AI prefers | Human writes |
|-----------|-------------|
| serves as | is |
| stands as | is |
| marks | is |
| represents | is |
| boasts | has |
| features | has |
| offers | has |

### Elegant Variation (Unnecessary Synonyms)

AI has a repetition-penalty that makes it avoid reusing words. It will give a character's name then switch to "the protagonist," "the key player," "the eponymous character" etc. If you mean the same thing, use the same word.

## Part 7: Content-Level AI Tells

### Undue Emphasis on Significance

LLM writing puffs up importance by adding statements about how aspects "represent" or "contribute to" broader topics.

**Words to watch:** stands/serves as, is a testament/reminder, a vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role/moment, underscores/highlights its importance/significance, reflects broader, symbolizing its, ongoing/enduring/lasting, contributing to the, setting the stage for, marking/shaping the, represents/marks a shift, key turning point, evolving landscape, focal point, indelible mark, deeply rooted

### Promotional and Advertisement-Like Language

LLMs have serious problems keeping a neutral tone, especially when writing about anything that could be considered "cultural heritage."

**Words to watch:** boasts a, vibrant, rich (figurative), profound, enhancing its, showcasing, exemplifies, commitment to, natural beauty, nestled, in the heart of, groundbreaking (figurative), renowned

### Superficial Analyses

AI inserts superficial analysis, often by attaching a present participle ("-ing") phrase at the end of sentences.

**Words to watch:** highlighting/underscoring/emphasizing..., ensuring..., reflecting/symbolizing..., contributing to..., cultivating/fostering... (figurative), encompassing..., valuable insights, align/resonate with

### Vague Attributions and Overgeneralization

AI attributes opinions or claims to vague authority.

**Words to watch:** Industry reports, Observers have cited, Experts argue, Some critics argue, several sources/publications (when only few are cited), such as (before exhaustive word lists)

### Knowledge-Cutoff Disclaimers

Phrases that leak the chatbot's awareness of its own limitations into prose:

- "as of [date]"
- "Up to my last training update"
- "as of my last knowledge update"
- "While specific details are limited/scarce..."
- "not widely available/documented/disclosed"
- "...in the provided/available sources/search results..."
- "based on available information..."

### Over-Signposting

Announcing what you're about to say instead of saying it:

- "In this section, we will discuss..."
- "This will include a discussion of..."
- "The purpose is to provide a comprehensive understanding..."
- "Now that we've explored X, let's move on to Y"

### Safe Conclusions

Zooming out to high-level generalizations:

- "As AI continues to evolve..."
- "The future remains bright for..."
- "Only time will tell..."
- Recap-only endings with no specific insight
- Paragraph that could work in any article on any topic

## Part 8: Adjective Audit Process

1. Strip all adjectives from a paragraph
2. Restore only those that change meaning usefully
3. Replace mood with measurement:
   - "robust system" -> "handles 10k requests/second without data loss"
   - "comprehensive solution" -> state what it actually covers
   - "innovative approach" -> describe what's new about it
4. Only use an adjective if it adds concrete information about scale, performance, or constraints

## Part 9: Two-Pass Revision Workflow

### Pass 1: Self-Critique

Before delivering prose, check:

- [ ] Three consecutive sentences match length? Break one.
- [ ] Paragraph ends with punchy one-liner? Vary it.
- [ ] Em-dash before a reveal? Remove it.
- [ ] Explaining a metaphor? Trust it to land.
- [ ] Any word from Tier 1 vocabulary list? Replace it.
- [ ] Any binary contrast structure? Flatten it.
- [ ] Rule of three? Cut to two or one.
- [ ] Generic opener or closer? Cut it.
- [ ] Same first 3-5 words on multiple sentences? Vary them.
- [ ] Any "serves as" / "stands as" constructions? Use "is."
- [ ] Present participle ("-ing") phrase tacked onto sentence end? Evaluate if needed.
- [ ] Self-referential meta-commentary? Remove it.

### Pass 2: Scoring

Rate 1-10 on each dimension:

| Dimension | Question |
|-----------|----------|
| Directness | Statements or announcements? |
| Rhythm | Varied or metronomic? |
| Trust | Respects reader intelligence? |
| Authenticity | Sounds human? |
| Density | Anything cuttable? |
| Specificity | Concrete or generic? |

Below 40/60: revise.

## Part 10: Before/After Examples

### Example 1: Throat-Clearing + Binary Contrast

**Before:**
> "Here's the thing: building products is hard. Not because the technology is complex. Because people are complex. Let that sink in."

**After:**
> "Building products is hard. Technology is manageable. People aren't."

### Example 2: Filler + Unnecessary Reassurance

**Before:**
> "It turns out that most teams struggle with alignment. The uncomfortable truth is that nobody wants to admit they're confused. And that's okay."

**After:**
> "Teams struggle with alignment. Nobody admits confusion."

### Example 3: Business Jargon Stack

**Before:**
> "In today's fast-paced landscape, we need to lean into discomfort and navigate uncertainty with clarity. This matters because your competition isn't waiting."

**After:**
> "Move faster. Your competition is."

### Example 4: Dramatic Fragmentation

**Before:**
> "Speed. Quality. Cost. You can only pick two. That's it. That's the tradeoff."

**After:**
> "Speed, quality, cost -- pick two."

### Example 5: Rhetorical Setup

**Before:**
> "What if I told you that the best teams don't optimize for productivity? Here's what I mean: they optimize for learning. Think about it."

**After:**
> "The best teams optimize for learning, not productivity."

### Example 6: Promotional Puffery (Wikipedia-style)

**Before:**
> "Nestled within the breathtaking region of Gonder in Ethiopia, Alamata Raya Kobo stands as a vibrant town with a rich cultural heritage and a significant place within the Amhara region. From its scenic landscapes to its historical landmarks, Alamata Raya Kobo offers visitors a fascinating glimpse into the diverse tapestry of Ethiopia."

**After:**
> "Alamata Raya Kobo is a town in the Amhara region of Ethiopia, in the Gonder area."

### Example 7: Superficial Analysis with -ing Phrases

**Before:**
> "The founding of Idescat represented a significant shift toward regional statistical independence, enabling Catalonia to develop a statistical system tailored to its unique socio-economic context. This initiative was part of a broader movement across Spain to decentralize administrative functions and enhance regional governance."

**After:**
> "Idescat gave Catalonia its own statistical system. Spain was decentralizing administrative functions at the time."

### Example 8: Overuse of Significance Words

**Before:**
> "This etymology highlights the enduring legacy of the community's resistance and the transformative power of unity in shaping its identity."

**After:**
> "The name reflects the community's history of resistance."

## Part 12: Structural Rules for Specific Content Types

### For Technical Writing / Documentation

- Prefer paragraphs over bullet lists for explanation
- Use bullet lists only for truly distinct items (steps, comparisons)
- Use subheadings sparingly, not for every paragraph
- Keep headings short and factual, not dramatic two-part constructions
- One consistent point of view ("we" or "you"), stick to it
- Full sentences, no stylistic fragments
- No analogies unless providing non-obvious clarification
- Maximum one analogy per piece
- Never "Imagine..." or "Think of it like this..."

### For Blog Posts / Articles

- No symmetric five-paragraph essay structure
- Vary section lengths
- Start with content, not with a question
- End with specific insight, not a generic zoom-out
- Use contractions naturally
- No more than one rhetorical question per piece (prefer zero)

### For Emails / Professional Communication

- "Any update on...?" not "I wanted to kindly follow up regarding..."
- Direct requests, minimal padding
- No "I hope this email finds you well"
- No "I would like to take a moment to"

## Part 13: The Core Insight

The fundamental difference between human and AI writing:

**AI deploys stylistic devices reflexively and indiscriminately. Humans use them intentionally and sparingly.**

Good writing varies structure. AI maintains unnatural consistency. Human authenticity comes from specificity rooted in tangible experience and developed voice.

The AI signature is "surface polish with nothing underneath" -- paragraphs that say in four sentences what needs one, coherent individually but lacking a throughline, plausible but never specific.

The antidote is always the same: be concrete, be specific, say less.
